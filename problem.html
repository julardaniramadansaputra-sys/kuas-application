Problem & Motivation

Why traditional maintenance fails and why a hybrid approach is necessary.

The Engineering Challenge

Power electronics, specifically DC-AC inverters, are identified as the most fragile components in modern renewable energy systems (e.g., Solar PV and Wind farms). Studies indicate that thermal cycling and power fluctuations cause accelerated aging in IGBT modules, leading to unexpected system downtime.

The core problem is not just the failure itself, but the inability to predict it accurately. Reactive maintenance (fixing after failure) is costly, while scheduled maintenance often results in replacing components that are still healthy (wasteful).

Current Technology Gap

Currently, two main approaches exist, but both have critical limitations for this specific application:

Limit 1: Pure Physics Models

Reliable but computationally expensive. They require precise parameters of the material properties, which change over time and are hard to measure in real-time operation.

Limit 2: Pure Data-Driven AI

"Black-box" nature. They require massive amounts of historical failure data to train effectively. In renewable energy, run-to-failure data is rare and expensive to collect.



Research Motivation

This research is motivated by the hypothesis that combining these two approaches can overcome their individual weaknesses. By using physics models to generate informative features (instead of raw data) and using AI to map these features to degradation trends, we can achieve high accuracy even with limited failure data.

Key Hypothesis: A Physics-Informed framework will provide better generalization on unseen data compared to standard Deep Learning models.
